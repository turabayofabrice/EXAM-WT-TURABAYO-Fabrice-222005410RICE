Our Approach

We strive for cultural and contextual sensitivity and act according to needs, resources and goals of the communities we work with. We orient our actions on research findings and international guidelines. Participation and the obligation to respect human rights have top priority.

Our Topics

We have put the topics of wellbeing and mental health, life under the impacts of climate change and social development through access to renewable energy at the core of our organisational purpose.

Our Projects

In Iraq, youths participate in creative and psychological activities to strengthen mental health and social connections and develop their visions of peaceful coexistence. In Germany, people with a migration background take part in guided peer support groups and shape their home in society. In South Sudan, Rwanda and Uganda, refugees are collaborating to develop sustainable energy possibilities for improvement of living conditions, well-being, safety and health in refugee camps. In Sierra Leone, communities are coming together to use biomass and the sun for energy, water and social development. In Kenya, innovative and environmentally-friendly urban mobility solutions lead to sustainable development. In Mayotte, bottom-up community initiatives are forming for access to energy and social development. In Madagascar, communities are forming around the water-food-energy nexus for socially sustainable development.

Writings

We attend to community narratives, documentation of daily experiences, perceptions and future ideas. We value and collect cultural productions and critical engagements of social movements that are often marginalised. We describe and analyse our activities and interventions and we draw attention to innovative new ways in wellbeing, mental health and adaptations to climate change.

Meet our colleague Josephine


Josephine Mwasaru, Hudara

For many people, interacting with nature is a tourist and adventurous experience, but for those with whom I share a similar background, it is a way of life and a means of livelihood. Growing up in my neighbourhood in a remote area in Kenya, I experienced the challenges of human-wildlife conflict and the depletion of the natural resources we depended on for survival. We lost our nearest source of water, the rains subsided, resulting in low or no harvests. Drought became rampant and wildlife often moved into areas of human habitat in search of food and water, leading to human-wildlife conflict. As a young girl, I never understood what was happening to our beautiful environment and why children had to skip school to get water for the household. Read more...
